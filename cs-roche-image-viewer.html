Roche Image Viewer

Led UX for a next-generation digital pathology image viewer used by pathologists worldwide at Roche.

Role Senior UX Designer
Timeline 2025
Platform Web Application

Overview

A viewer built for the demands of clinical pathology

Roche's digital pathology platform enables pathologists to examine whole slide images (WSIs) — gigapixel scans of tissue specimens — entirely on screen rather than under a microscope. At the heart of that platform is the image viewer: the tool pathologists spend the majority of their diagnostic time inside.

The challenge was to design a high-performance viewer that could handle the scale of these images without compromising responsiveness, while surfacing the clinical tools and metadata pathologists need exactly when they need them. The viewer had to work equally well for routine diagnosis, tumour boards, and multi-site telepathology consultations.

Problem

Cluttered interfaces were stealing time from diagnosis

Existing digital pathology viewers had evolved by layering feature upon feature without a coherent interaction model. The result was dense toolbars, inconsistent zoom controls, and annotation workflows buried several clicks deep. Pathologists were spending measurable portions of their session time simply navigating the interface rather than examining tissue.

Interviews with pathologists across Europe and the US surfaced a consistent frustration: the tools felt designed for software engineers, not clinicians. Critical functions like switching between stain channels, comparing previous cases, or adding diagnostic annotations required too many steps. In a high-volume lab environment, that friction compounded across hundreds of cases each week.

Process

Research-led design across three countries

The project began with a structured discovery phase — contextual interviews and observational sessions with pathologists at clinical sites in Germany, the UK, and the United States. I mapped their diagnostic workflows end-to-end, identifying where the interface created friction and where cognitive load was highest.

Using those insights, I ran a series of co-design workshops to explore navigation models and toolbar architectures. Concepts were rapidly prototyped in Figma and tested in moderated sessions with 12 pathologist participants across the three countries. Each round of testing directly informed the next iteration, compressing the feedback loop between research and design.

Key decisions — such as the contextual toolbar that surfaces only the tools relevant to the current task, and the persistent thumbnail navigator for large WSIs — were validated against measurable task-completion criteria before being committed to engineering.

Outcomes

Measurable impact at scale

The redesigned viewer shipped to clinical sites across Europe, the US, and Asia-Pacific. Post-launch measurement confirmed significant improvements in diagnostic efficiency and user satisfaction, with the product receiving strong adoption across new site deployments.

40% reduction in task time
